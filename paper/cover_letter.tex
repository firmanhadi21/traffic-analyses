\opening{Dear Editors,}

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, ``Spatiotemporal Analysis of Urban Traffic Congestion,
Network Topology, and Capacity Constraints in Indonesian Metropolitan Cities,'' for
consideration in \emph{Transportation Research Interdisciplinary Perspectives}.

\textbf{Relevance to the journal.}
This manuscript bridges multiple transportation research domains---spatial analysis, network
science, traffic engineering, and urban planning---making it a natural fit for TRIP's
interdisciplinary scope. A central outcome of this work is a well-evidenced null result:
four independent spatial predictors fail to explain congestion variance, while temporal
factors dominate by three orders of magnitude. We believe this finding aligns with TRIP's
stated openness to negative and controversial results, and carries important policy
implications for rapidly urbanising cities in the Global South.

\textbf{Summary.}
We analyze 264 million traffic observations collected at 15-minute intervals over 11 months
across three Indonesian metropolitan cities---Jakarta (metro pop.\ 34M), Bandung (2.5M), and
Semarang (1.8M)---spanning a 20$\times$ population range. Using an open-source Python pipeline
that integrates the HERE Traffic API, OSMnx, and PySAL, we combine geostatistical methods
(Moran's I, LISA, Getis-Ord Gi*), network topology analysis, and a novel graph-based capacity
drop detection framework.

Our primary finding is that time-of-day ($\eta^2$ = 15--24\%) explains 1,000--4,000$\times$
more congestion variance than any spatial predictor---including network centrality, POI density,
road capacity, and bottleneck proximity (all $R^2$ < 0.01\%). H3 hexagonal aggregation at
multiple scales confirms these null findings are not modifiable areal unit artefacts. We argue
that congestion is fundamentally a demand synchronization problem, supporting demand management
interventions (congestion pricing, flexible work schedules, transit expansion) over capacity
expansion alone.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item The first systematic multi-city comparison of temporal versus spatial congestion predictors
in Southeast Asia, using continuous high-resolution monitoring data.
\item A novel graph-based capacity drop detection module using directed OSMnx road networks.
\item Evidence that normalized congestion metrics (e.g., jam factor) inherently remove capacity
effects, with methodological implications for capacity--congestion studies.
\item A fully open, installable Python pipeline (\texttt{pip install traffic-congestion-pipeline})
bridging commercial traffic data with the OSMnx/PySAL ecosystem.
\end{enumerate}
