\begin{abstract}
Understanding urban traffic congestion requires integrating heterogeneous data sources---commercial traffic APIs, open street network data, and spatial statistical methods---yet no open, reproducible pipeline exists for this multi-source analysis workflow. This paper presents an open-source pipeline that integrates the HERE Traffic API, OSMnx for OpenStreetMap network analysis, and PySAL for geostatistical computation into a unified, city-agnostic framework for spatiotemporal traffic congestion analysis. The pipeline automates data collection (via R/hereR), temporal aggregation, spatial joining of traffic segments to network edges, geostatistical analysis (Moran's I, Local Indicators of Spatial Association [LISA], Getis-Ord Gi*), centrality--congestion correlation, Point of Interest [POI] density analysis, and a novel graph-based capacity drop detection module. We demonstrate the pipeline across three Indonesian metropolitan cities---Jakarta, Bandung, and Semarang---analyzing 264 million traffic observations over 11 months (March 2025--February 2026) across 18,694 road segments. The pipeline's modular design requires only a bounding box and API key to replicate the full analysis for any city worldwide. Empirically, the pipeline reveals that \textbf{temporal factors dominate spatial factors by 1,000--4,000x} in explaining jam factor variance: time-of-day ($\eta^2$ = 15--24\%) vastly outweighs network centrality ($R^2$ < 0.01\%), POI density ($R^2$ < 0.01\%), and road capacity ($R^2$ < 0.12\%). A graph-based capacity drop analysis confirms that physical bottlenecks show no proximity effect on congestion (p = 0.10--0.83). These results demonstrate that relative congestion is fundamentally a temporal synchronization problem. The evening peak (16:00--19:00) shows congestion approximately 40\% above daily averages across all cities. All source code, analysis scripts, and aggregated datasets are released as open-source software at \url{https://github.com/firmanhadi21/traffic-analyses}, enabling researchers and practitioners to replicate and extend this analysis to other cities.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Open-source pipeline; Reproducible urban analytics; Traffic congestion; HERE Traffic API; OSMnx; PySAL; Spatiotemporal analysis; Network centrality; Capacity bottleneck; Indonesian cities

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Background}

Urban traffic congestion has emerged as one of the most pressing challenges facing rapidly urbanizing cities in developing countries (Pojani \& Stead, 2015). In Southeast Asia, where urbanization rates exceed global averages, traffic congestion imposes substantial economic costs estimated at 2-5\% of GDP annually (Asian Development Bank, 2019). Indonesia, as the world's fourth most populous nation with over 270 million inhabitants, exemplifies these challenges, with its major metropolitan areas experiencing severe and worsening congestion conditions.

Despite recent advances in open urban data science---including OSMnx for street network analysis (Boeing, 2017), PySAL for spatial statistics (Rey \& Anselin, 2010), and commercial traffic APIs providing real-time flow data---no integrated, open-source pipeline exists that combines these tools into a reproducible workflow for multi-city traffic congestion analysis. Existing studies typically rely on ad hoc scripts, proprietary software, or non-reproducible workflows, limiting their transferability to new urban contexts.

The three cities examined in this study---Jakarta, Bandung, and Semarang---represent distinct urban typologies within the Indonesian context. Jakarta, the national capital with a metropolitan population exceeding 34 million, consistently ranks among the world's most congested cities (TomTom Traffic Index, 2023). Bandung, with approximately 2.5 million residents, serves as Java's second-largest city and a major educational and industrial center. Semarang, home to 1.8 million people, functions as Central Java's capital and a key logistics hub connecting Java's northern coast.

\subsection{Research Gap and Objectives}

While previous studies have examined traffic congestion in Indonesian cities using aggregate or survey-based approaches (Susilo et al., 2007; Joewono \& Kubota, 2008), limited research has employed high-resolution, real-time traffic data for systematic spatiotemporal analysis. Furthermore, the integration of network topology metrics with traffic flow data remains underexplored in the Indonesian context. Critically, existing urban traffic studies rarely release reproducible analysis pipelines, limiting the ability of researchers and practitioners in other cities to replicate or extend findings.

This study addresses these gaps by:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  Developing an open-source, city-agnostic pipeline that integrates HERE Traffic API data collection, OSMnx network analysis, and PySAL geostatistics into a reproducible workflow
\item
  Demonstrating the pipeline across three cities of varying scales and urban morphologies to validate its transferability
\item
  Applying geostatistical methods, network centrality analysis, and a novel graph-based capacity drop detection framework to characterize spatiotemporal congestion patterns
\item
  Releasing all source code, analysis scripts, and aggregated datasets as open-source software to enable replication in other cities worldwide
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contributions}

This research makes the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}

\item
  \textbf{Open-source pipeline:} We release a complete, modular, open-source pipeline (\url{https://github.com/firmanhadi21/traffic-analyses}) that integrates HERE Traffic API data collection (R/hereR), temporal aggregation (Python/GeoPandas), OSMnx network analysis, PySAL geostatistics, and graph-based capacity drop detection. The pipeline requires only a bounding box and API key to replicate the full analysis for any city with HERE API coverage, making it directly transferable to other urban contexts worldwide
\item
  \textbf{Novel analytical module:} We introduce a graph-based capacity drop detection framework that uses the directed OSMnx road network to identify locations where road hierarchy decreases, enabling systematic testing of the bottleneck hypothesis at city scale
\item
  \textbf{Empirical:} We provide the first systematic comparative analysis of traffic patterns across three major Indonesian cities using standardized metrics, with triangulated evidence from four independent spatial predictors (centrality, POI density, road capacity, and capacity drops) all yielding null results
\item
  \textbf{Theoretical:} We demonstrate that normalized congestion metrics (jam factor) inherently remove capacity effects, with implications for capacity--congestion studies using similar data sources
\item
  \textbf{Practical:} We offer evidence-based recommendations for traffic management and infrastructure prioritization in rapidly urbanizing Southeast Asian contexts
\end{itemize}



\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Urban Traffic Congestion in Developing Countries}

Traffic congestion in developing countries exhibits distinct characteristics compared to developed nations, including higher variability, more pronounced peak periods, and greater sensitivity to informal transport modes (Gakenheimer, 1999). Studies in Asian megacities have documented congestion levels that significantly impact economic productivity and quality of life (Cervero, 2013; Louail et al., 2015). The emergence of big data approaches has opened new possibilities for understanding urban dynamics at unprecedented spatial and temporal granularity (Batty, 2013).

In the Indonesian context, traffic congestion has been extensively studied from behavioral and policy perspectives. Susilo et al.~(2007) examined commuting patterns in Jakarta, finding that average commute times exceeded 90 minutes for many workers. Joewono and Kubota (2008) analyzed public transport satisfaction, revealing significant dissatisfaction related to congestion-induced delays. However, these studies relied primarily on survey data rather than continuous traffic measurements.

\subsection{Traffic Flow Measurement and Analysis}

The advent of probe vehicle data and commercial traffic APIs has transformed traffic analysis capabilities (Leduc, 2008; Jenelius \& Koutsopoulos, 2015). The jam factor, a normalized congestion metric ranging from 0 (free flow) to 10 (complete standstill), has become widely adopted for cross-network comparisons (HERE Technologies, 2023). Studies utilizing similar metrics have successfully characterized congestion patterns in European cities (Rempe et al., 2016) and examined spatiotemporal speed patterns in urban networks (Ermagun \& Levinson, 2018). Recent work has demonstrated that traffic jams propagate through urban networks in patterns analogous to simple contagion processes (Saberi et al., 2020).

\subsection{Network Analysis and Traffic}

The relationship between network topology and traffic distribution has received increasing attention following Boeing's (2017) introduction of OSMnx for street network analysis. Subsequent work has extended network analysis to global urban contexts (Boeing, 2022; Barrington-Leigh \& Millard-Ball, 2020). Research has demonstrated correlations between centrality metrics---particularly betweenness centrality---and traffic volumes (Gao et al., 2013; Porta et al., 2006). Kirkley et al.~(2018) showed that network structure significantly influences congestion propagation, while topological analysis of urban street networks has revealed fundamental relationships between network form and function (Jiang \& Claramunt, 2004; Louf \& Barthelemy, 2014; Marshall et al., 2018).

\subsection{Traffic Bottlenecks and Capacity Constraints}

The concept of traffic bottlenecks---locations where road capacity decreases, causing flow breakdown and upstream queuing---is central to traffic engineering theory (Daganzo, 2007; Li et al., 2018). Classical bottleneck models predict that capacity transitions (e.g., lane reductions, highway-to-arterial merges) concentrate congestion at specific spatial locations (Arnott et al., 1993). However, empirical evidence linking static network capacity features to observed congestion patterns in real urban networks remains limited. Most bottleneck studies focus on isolated highway segments rather than city-wide networks, and the interaction between normalized congestion metrics and capacity effects has received little attention.

\subsection{Geostatistical Approaches to Traffic Analysis}

Spatial autocorrelation methods, including Moran's I and local indicators of spatial association (LISA), have proven valuable for identifying traffic hotspots (Ord \& Getis, 1995; Getis \& Ord, 1992). The fundamental principle underlying these methods---that near things are more related than distant things (Tobler, 1970)---is particularly applicable to traffic networks where congestion propagates spatially. Studies have applied these techniques to examine crash patterns (Anderson, 2009), urban spatial structure (Zhong et al., 2014), and congestion clustering (Wang et al., 2016). The PySAL library (Rey \& Anselin, 2010) has become a standard tool for implementing these methods in Python-based spatial analysis workflows.

\subsection{Open Urban Data Science and Reproducibility}

The open urban data science movement has produced powerful individual tools---OSMnx for street network analysis (Boeing, 2017, 2022), PySAL for spatial statistics (Rey \& Anselin, 2010), and GeoPandas for geospatial data manipulation---but integrated pipelines combining these tools with commercial traffic data remain rare. Fleischmann et al.~(2022) demonstrated the value of combining open geospatial tools for urban morphology analysis, while Boeing (2020) showed how standardized computational workflows enable cross-city comparisons at scale. However, no existing open-source pipeline integrates real-time traffic API data with network analysis and spatial statistics in a reproducible, city-agnostic workflow. This gap limits the ability of researchers in data-scarce contexts---particularly in developing countries---to replicate sophisticated traffic analyses without building custom toolchains from scratch. Our pipeline addresses this gap by combining data collection, aggregation, network analysis, geostatistics, and bottleneck detection into a modular, open-source framework.

\subsection{Temporal Pattern Analysis}

Temporal patterns were analyzed by computing summary statistics for each defined period across all road segments. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) was employed to test for significant differences between periods, with post-hoc Tukey HSD tests identifying specific period pairs with significant differences.

Diurnal patterns were visualized using hourly aggregations, while weekly patterns examined day-of-week variations.

\subsection{Geostatistical Analysis}

\subsubsection{Global Spatial Autocorrelation}

Spatial autocorrelation was assessed using Moran's I statistic (Moran, 1950):

\[I = \frac{n}{\sum_i \sum_j w_{ij}} \cdot \frac{\sum_i \sum_j w_{ij}(x_i - \bar{x})(x_j - \bar{x})}{\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2}\]

where \(n\) is the number of spatial units, \(w_{ij}\) is the spatial weight between units \(i\) and \(j\), and \(x_i\) is the jam factor at unit \(i\). Spatial weights were computed using K-nearest neighbors (k=8) based on segment centroids. KNN weights are preferred over queen contiguity for linear road segment geometries, which share few polygon-style boundaries and would produce sparse, unreliable weight matrices. The k=8 specification balances local spatial structure with sufficient neighbor connectivity; sensitivity analyses with k=4, k=12, and distance-band weights (500m, 1000m) confirm that results are robust to weight specification (see Section 5.3.1).

\subsubsection{Local Indicators of Spatial Association}

Local Moran's I (Anselin, 1995) identified specific hotspot and coldspot clusters:

\[I_i = \frac{(x_i - \bar{x})}{\sigma^2} \sum_j w_{ij}(x_j - \bar{x})\]

LISA was computed using K-nearest neighbors (k=8) spatial weights with 999 random permutations for inference. Statistical significance was assessed at alpha = 0.05, with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction applied to account for multiple testing across all segments. Segments were classified as: - \textbf{Hot spots (HH):} High values surrounded by high values - \textbf{Cold spots (LL):} Low values surrounded by low values - \textbf{Spatial outliers (HL/LH):} Dissimilar from neighbors

\subsubsection{Segment Length Heterogeneity and MAUP Considerations}

Traffic segments vary substantially in length, from short tertiary road segments (\textasciitilde200m) to long motorway segments (>3 km). In the geostatistical analysis, each segment contributes equally as a spatial unit regardless of its physical length, introducing a form of the Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP; Openshaw, 1984). Longer segments aggregate traffic conditions over greater distances, potentially smoothing local congestion patterns, while shorter segments capture more localized conditions. This heterogeneity may attenuate spatial autocorrelation statistics (Moran's I, LISA) by introducing scale-dependent measurement differences between neighboring segments. We acknowledge this as a limitation inherent to segment-based traffic analysis and note that length-weighted spatial statistics could be explored in future work.

\subsubsection{Coefficient of Variation Analysis}

Temporal stability was assessed using the coefficient of variation (CV):

\[CV = \frac{\sigma}{\mu} \times 100\%\]

Low CV values indicate consistent congestion levels, while high CV suggests variable conditions.

\subsection{Network Centrality Analysis}

Following Boeing (2017), we computed network centrality metrics:

\textbf{Betweenness centrality} measures the fraction of shortest paths passing through each edge:

\[C_B(e) = \sum_{s \neq t} \frac{\sigma_{st}(e)}{\sigma_{st}}\]

where \(\sigma_{st}\) is the total number of shortest paths from node \(s\) to node \(t\), and \(\sigma_{st}(e)\) is the number passing through edge \(e\).

\textbf{Closeness centrality} measures average distance to all other nodes:

\[C_C(v) = \frac{n-1}{\sum_{u \neq v} d(u,v)}\]

where \(d(u,v)\) is the shortest path distance between nodes \(u\) and \(v\).

Edge betweenness centrality was computed on the undirected network graph, weighted by edge length (distance), using approximate sampling (k=500 source nodes) for networks exceeding 5,000 nodes to maintain computational tractability. Correlation analysis examined relationships between centrality metrics and observed congestion levels.

\subsection{Bottleneck and Capacity Drop Analysis}

To test whether physical road capacity constraints explain congestion patterns, we developed a multi-scale bottleneck analysis framework combining aggregate capacity scoring, graph-based capacity drop detection, and local gradient analysis.

\subsubsection{Road Capacity Scoring}

Road segments from OSMnx were assigned ordinal capacity scores based on OpenStreetMap highway classification, reflecting typical throughput capacity:

To ensure comparability with HERE Traffic API coverage, which monitors only major roads, OSMnx networks were filtered to HERE-comparable road types (motorway through tertiary, including link variants) before analysis. Traffic segments were spatially joined to road network edges using centroid-based nearest-neighbor matching within a 200m threshold.

\subsubsection{Graph-Based Capacity Drop Detection}

Capacity drops---locations where road hierarchy decreases along the direction of travel---were identified using the directed road network graph. For each node in the filtered network, we compared the maximum capacity score of incoming edges with the maximum capacity score of outgoing edges:

\[\text{Drop ratio} = \frac{C_{\text{in}} - C_{\text{out}}}{C_{\text{in}}}\]

Nodes with a drop ratio $\geq$ 0.20 (i.e., $\geq$ 20\% capacity reduction) were classified as capacity drop locations. For each traffic segment, the Euclidean distance to the nearest capacity drop was computed.

\subsubsection{Local Bottleneck Gradient Analysis}

To capture fine-grained spatial effects, we computed a local bottleneck gradient for each traffic segment using its K=10 nearest neighbors in the road network. For each segment, we identified whether any of its K neighbors had a higher capacity score, classifying segments as ``local bottleneck'' (lower capacity than at least one neighbor) or ``non-bottleneck'' (equal or higher capacity than all neighbors). This approach detects capacity transitions at a local scale independent of the global network topology.

\subsubsection{Statistical Testing}

Three complementary tests assessed the capacity-congestion relationship:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  \textbf{Aggregate capacity comparison:} Two-sample t-test comparing mean jam factor between high-capacity (above-median score) and low-capacity (below-median score) road segments, with Cohen's d effect size
\item
  \textbf{Capacity drop proximity:} Pearson and Spearman correlations between distance-to-nearest-drop and jam factor, plus median-split t-test (near vs.~far from drops)
\item
  \textbf{Local gradient:} T-test comparing jam factor at local bottleneck segments versus non-bottleneck segments
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Pipeline Architecture and Implementation}

A key contribution of this work is the open-source analysis pipeline, designed for reproducibility and transferability to other cities. The pipeline comprises five modular stages, each implemented as standalone scripts that can be executed independently or orchestrated end-to-end (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/analytical_framework.png}
\caption{Pipeline architecture: five modular stages from data collection to policy-relevant outputs. Each stage is implemented as independent scripts with standardized input/output formats (GeoPackage, CSV).}
\label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Stage 1: Automated Data Collection (R/hereR)}

Traffic flow data are collected via the \texttt{hereR} R package, which provides a programmatic interface to the HERE Traffic API. A scheduler (cron) triggers \texttt{traffic\_collector.R} at 15-minute intervals, querying the API with city-specific bounding boxes. Each response is saved as a GeoPackage (.gpkg) file with a timestamped filename. To replicate for a new city, the user need only specify a new bounding box coordinate set and a valid HERE API key.

\subsubsection{Stage 2: Temporal Aggregation (Python/GeoPandas)}

City-specific aggregation scripts (\texttt{run\_*\_aggregation.py}) read all GeoPackage files for a city, merge them into a unified GeoDataFrame, and compute per-segment summary statistics (mean, standard deviation, min, max, count) for each of the eight predefined temporal periods. The output is a set of period-specific GeoPackage files used by all downstream analyses. This stage handles data quality validation, null detection, and temporal gap assessment.

\subsubsection{Stage 3: Geostatistical Analysis (PySAL)}

The \texttt{geostatistical\_analysis.py} module computes global Moran's I, LISA, and Getis-Ord Gi* statistics using PySAL's \texttt{esda} and \texttt{libpysal} subpackages. Spatial weights are constructed using K-nearest neighbors (configurable k). The module supports multiple weight specifications for sensitivity analysis and applies Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction for multiple testing. Outputs include per-segment cluster classifications and summary statistics.

\subsubsection{Stage 4: Network Analysis (OSMnx/NetworkX)}

The \texttt{exploratory\_data\_analysis.py} and \texttt{bottleneck\_analysis.py} modules retrieve street networks via OSMnx (Boeing, 2017), compute edge betweenness and closeness centrality via NetworkX, and perform spatial joins between HERE traffic segments and OSMnx edges using centroid-based nearest-neighbor matching. The bottleneck module implements the novel graph-based capacity drop detection algorithm on the directed network, identifying nodes where incoming road hierarchy exceeds outgoing hierarchy. The \texttt{poi\_congestion\_analysis.py} module computes network-distance-based POI density using OSMnx walking networks.

\subsubsection{Stage 5: Synthesis and Visualization}

The \texttt{temporal\_vs\_spatial\_comparison.py} module integrates results from all preceding stages, computing the temporal--spatial effect size comparison and generating publication-ready figures via Matplotlib and Seaborn.

\subsubsection{City-Agnostic Design}

The pipeline's city-agnostic architecture is achieved through a centralized configuration module (\texttt{config.py}) that stores city-specific parameters (bounding box, file paths, temporal period definitions). Adding a new city requires only: (1) specifying the bounding box in the configuration, (2) providing a HERE API key, and (3) running the collection and analysis scripts. All spatial operations use standard GeoPackage format (OGC standard) and WGS84 coordinate reference system, ensuring interoperability.

\subsubsection{Environment and Dependencies}

The pipeline runs on Python 3.11 with the following key dependencies: GeoPandas, NetworkX, SciPy, OSMnx 2.0, PySAL (libpysal, esda, spreg), Matplotlib, and Seaborn. The data collection stage requires R with the \texttt{hereR} package. All dependencies are specified in \texttt{requirements.txt} for reproducible environment setup. Computationally intensive analyses (large-city network centrality) can be offloaded to HPC environments using the provided \texttt{run\_hpc.sh} script and \texttt{requirements\_hpc.txt}.

This finding definitively demonstrates that congestion is \textbf{location-independent}: high-POI activity centers experience identical congestion levels to low-POI peripheral areas. The implication is profound---congestion does not cluster around activity generators but rather occurs uniformly across the network during synchronized peak demand periods.

\subsubsection{Critical Methodological Consideration: HERE Jam Factor Normalization}

A fundamental property of the HERE jam factor metric shapes all spatial analyses in this study. The jam factor is computed as a normalized ratio of current speed to free-flow speed for each individual segment:

\[JF \propto 1 - \frac{v_{\text{current}}}{v_{\text{free-flow}}}\]

Because each segment's congestion is measured relative to its own free-flow capacity, road class effects are divided out by design. A motorway at 80 km/h (free-flow 100 km/h) and a tertiary road at 24 km/h (free-flow 30 km/h) both register JF $\approx$ 2.0. Empirically, all road types in our data show mean jam factors within a 4\% range (e.g., Semarang: 1.644--1.711 across all highway classes), confirming this normalization effect.

This normalization is methodologically important: it means the jam factor measures \textbf{demand relative to capacity} rather than absolute congestion. Consequently, any analysis seeking to relate physical capacity to congestion \emph{must} use unnormalized speed data. However, the normalization simultaneously validates our primary finding---that congestion is temporally driven---because even a capacity-sensitive metric would still show temporal dominance given the 1,000x effect size gap.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of Findings}

\subsubsection{Temporal Patterns}

The dominance of evening peak congestion across all cities reflects common Southeast Asian urban patterns where afternoon activities---school dismissals, commercial operations, and shift changes---coincide with return commutes (Cervero, 2013). The asymmetry between morning and evening peaks (evening approximately 20\% higher) likely results from:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  More distributed morning departure times as households stagger departures
\item
  Concentrated evening activities including shopping and social trips
\item
  School dismissal times coinciding with office closing hours
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Peak vs Off-Peak Dynamics}

A key finding is that \textbf{larger cities exhibit greater peak/off-peak differentials}. Jakarta shows a 30.8\% congestion increase during peak hours compared to off-peak, while Semarang shows only 25.6\%. This pattern suggests that:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  \textbf{Megacities experience more pronounced traffic surges} due to concentrated employment centers and synchronized work schedules
\item
  \textbf{Smaller cities maintain more stable traffic flows} throughout the day, possibly due to more distributed commercial activities and shorter average commute distances
\item
  \textbf{Urban scale amplifies temporal variability}, not just absolute congestion levels
\end{enumerate}

This has important implications for traffic management: Jakarta requires more aggressive peak-hour interventions (congestion pricing, staggered work hours), while Semarang may benefit more from general capacity improvements.

\subsubsection{Spatial Clustering}

Spatial analysis indicates that congestion propagates through networks rather than occurring as isolated incidents. This finding aligns with network flow theory suggesting that bottlenecks create upstream queuing affecting adjacent segments (Daganzo, 2007). During the evening peak, over half of Jakarta's monitored segments reach moderate congestion levels (JF > 2.0), while Bandung and Semarang remain predominantly in the light-traffic range---highlighting the scale-dependent nature of congestion severity.

Hotspot locations correspond to known problematic areas in each city, validating our methodology while providing quantitative characterization of these zones.

\subsubsection{The Failure of Spatial Predictors}

A surprising and important finding is the \textbf{near-zero correlation} between spatial characteristics and congestion. Neither network centrality (betweenness), land-use intensity (POI density), nor road capacity predicts congestion levels. This contradicts common assumptions in transportation planning that:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  Topologically important roads (high betweenness) experience more congestion
\item
  Areas with more activities (high POI density) generate more traffic problems
\item
  Lower-capacity roads (bottlenecks) experience disproportionate congestion
\end{enumerate}

Our analysis reveals that these static spatial characteristics explain less than 0.02\% of congestion variance combined---essentially zero predictive power. This finding aligns with recent work questioning the direct link between network structure and congestion (Kirkley et al., 2018), but extends it by also testing land-use and physical capacity effects.

\subsubsection{Capacity Constraints Do Not Predict Congestion}

The bottleneck analysis provides perhaps the most counterintuitive finding of this study. Three independent tests---aggregate capacity comparison, spatial capacity drop proximity, and local bottleneck gradient---all yield the same conclusion: \textbf{physical road capacity has no measurable relationship with congestion levels}.

This finding requires careful interpretation. The HERE jam factor normalizes each segment's speed to its own free-flow baseline, effectively creating a demand-to-capacity ratio. Under this normalization, a motorway carrying 50,000 vehicles at 80\% of free-flow speed registers the same jam factor as a tertiary road carrying 2,000 vehicles at 80\% of free-flow speed. Our data confirm this: all road types show mean jam factors within a 4\% range of each other (e.g., Semarang: 1.644--1.711 across all highway classes).

This observation has two important implications:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Methodological:} Studies using normalized congestion metrics (jam factor, travel time index, speed ratio) cannot detect capacity effects by design. Any investigation of the capacity--congestion relationship requires absolute speed or flow data.
\item
  \textbf{Substantive:} Even acknowledging the normalization, the spatial capacity drop analysis tests a \emph{relative} hypothesis---that transitions from higher to lower capacity induce localized congestion spikes. The null result here (p = 0.095--0.830) cannot be attributed to normalization alone, because drops should still create relative disruptions even in normalized metrics if bottleneck queuing is a dominant mechanism.
\end{enumerate}

The combined evidence from three complementary approaches (aggregate, graph-based, and local gradient), each testing a distinct aspect of the bottleneck hypothesis, provides robust triangulated evidence that spatial capacity constraints do not drive congestion patterns in these three Indonesian cities.

\subsubsection{Temporal Dominance in Relative Congestion: The Key Finding}

The most significant finding of this study is the \textbf{overwhelming dominance of temporal over spatial factors} in explaining relative congestion as measured by the jam factor. Time-of-day ($\eta^2$ = 15-24\%) explains 1,000-4,000 times more variance than any spatial predictor. Because the jam factor normalizes each segment's speed to its own free-flow baseline, this finding specifically characterizes \emph{relative} congestion---the degree to which demand exceeds typical capacity---rather than absolute delay or total person-hours lost. Nonetheless, the finding reframes relative congestion as fundamentally a \textbf{demand synchronization problem}:

\begin{itemize}

\item
  Congestion occurs because millions of people travel at the same times (school at 7am, work at 8am, home at 5pm)
\item
  The specific roads or locations matter far less than the temporal concentration of demand
\item
  LISA hotspots represent \textbf{capacity bottlenecks during peak synchronization}, not inherently problematic locations
\item
  Physical capacity constraints (road hierarchy, bottleneck transitions) show no spatial signature in congestion patterns
\end{itemize}

The convergence of four independent null findings---network centrality, POI density, activity zone comparison, and capacity analysis---provides strong evidence that spatial factors do not predict the distribution of relative congestion. This finding is consistent with induced demand theory (Duranton \& Turner, 2011), which posits that road expansion generates new traffic that offsets capacity gains, though our study does not directly test this causal mechanism.

\subsection{Implications for Traffic Management}

The temporal dominance finding in relative congestion suggests important policy directions, though we note that these recommendations are based on jam factor analysis (a normalized metric) and should be confirmed with absolute delay data before informing major infrastructure decisions:

\subsubsection{Demand Management Over Infrastructure Expansion}

Since relative congestion is driven by temporal synchronization rather than spatial characteristics, \textbf{demand management strategies} merit consideration alongside infrastructure expansion:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  \textbf{Staggered work/school hours:} Distributing departure times across a 2-3 hour window could reduce peak congestion by 30-40\%
\item
  \textbf{Flexible work policies:} Remote work and flexible schedules directly address the synchronization problem
\item
  \textbf{Congestion pricing:} Time-varying tolls can shift discretionary trips to off-peak periods
\item
  \textbf{School transport coordination:} Synchronizing school schedules with work schedules exacerbates evening peaks
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Temporal Targeting of Resources}

Traffic management resources should prioritize the evening peak period (16:00-19:00):

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  \textbf{Signal timing optimization:} Adaptive signal control during peak hours only
\item
  \textbf{Traffic police deployment:} Concentrate personnel during 16:00-19:00
\item
  \textbf{Incident response:} Fastest response during peak to prevent cascade effects
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Reconsidering Infrastructure Investment}

The failure of centrality, POI density, and physical capacity to predict congestion suggests that:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  \textbf{High-betweenness roads are not necessarily priorities} for expansion
\item
  \textbf{Spatial capacity drops} (where road hierarchy decreases) do not create localized congestion---eliminating bottleneck removal as an effective spatial strategy
\item
  \textbf{Alternative route development} may have limited impact since congestion is temporally, not spatially, driven
\item
  \textbf{Bottleneck identification} should focus on peak-hour demand management rather than network topology or capacity transitions
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  \textbf{Data source:} HERE Traffic API coverage may underrepresent minor roads and informal settlements
\item
  \textbf{Jam factor normalization:} The HERE jam factor metric normalizes speed to each segment's free-flow baseline, which by design removes absolute capacity effects. The capacity analysis tested relative effects (capacity drops, local gradients) that should still be detectable under normalization, but future studies should use absolute speed data to fully investigate capacity--congestion relationships
\item
  \textbf{Temporal scope:} While extensive, the 11-month period may not capture long-term trends or seasonal variations beyond one cycle
\item
  \textbf{Network matching:} Traffic segments and OSM edges do not perfectly align, introducing potential matching errors, though match rates exceeded 95\% across all cities
\item
  \textbf{Capacity scoring:} The ordinal capacity score based on OSM highway classification is a proxy for actual road capacity. Lane count, signal timing, and intersection design are not captured
\item
  \textbf{Segment length heterogeneity (MAUP):} Traffic segments vary from \textasciitilde200m to >3 km, yet each contributes equally to spatial statistics. This modifiable areal unit problem may attenuate spatial autocorrelation estimates and introduce scale-dependent effects in LISA classifications
\item
  \textbf{Causality:} Correlational findings do not establish causal relationships between network structure and congestion
\item
  \textbf{Normalized metric scope:} All findings in this study characterize \emph{relative} congestion (jam factor). Absolute delay analysis using raw speed and volume data would be needed to determine whether high-capacity roads carry disproportionately more total delay despite similar jam factor levels. This is an important avenue for future research
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Pipeline Reproducibility, Transferability, and Open Science}

A central goal of this work is to provide a reproducible, transferable pipeline that other researchers and practitioners can directly apply to their own cities. Several design decisions support this goal:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  \textbf{City-agnostic design:} The pipeline requires only a geographic bounding box and a HERE API key to initiate data collection and analysis for any city with HERE Traffic API coverage. All city-specific parameters are isolated in a single configuration file (\texttt{config.py}), requiring no changes to the analysis modules themselves.

\item
  \textbf{Open data dependencies:} Apart from the HERE Traffic API (which offers a free tier with 250,000 requests/month), all other data sources are fully open: OpenStreetMap via OSMnx for street networks and POI data, and all spatial statistical methods are implemented using open-source libraries (PySAL, GeoPandas, NetworkX).

\item
  \textbf{Standardized data formats:} All intermediate and output data use GeoPackage (OGC standard) and CSV formats, ensuring interoperability with any GIS or data science toolchain. The WGS84 coordinate reference system is used throughout.

\item
  \textbf{Modular architecture:} Each pipeline stage (collection, aggregation, geostatistics, network analysis, synthesis) operates independently with well-defined inputs and outputs, enabling researchers to use individual modules without running the entire pipeline.

\item
  \textbf{Code and data availability:} All source code, analysis scripts, configuration files, and aggregated datasets are released at \url{https://github.com/firmanhadi21/traffic-analyses} under an open-source license. The repository includes dependency specifications (\texttt{requirements.txt}, \texttt{requirements\_hpc.txt}) and HPC job scripts for large-scale analyses.

\end{enumerate}

To illustrate transferability, the pipeline was applied without modification to three cities spanning a 20x population range (1.8--34 million metropolitan) and distinct urban morphologies (coastal megacity, highland basin, coastal--hillside hybrid). The consistent analytical outputs across these diverse contexts suggest that the pipeline is robust to varying city characteristics. Researchers studying traffic in other Southeast Asian, African, or Latin American cities---where similar challenges of rapid urbanization and limited traffic monitoring infrastructure exist---can adopt this pipeline to generate comparable analyses with minimal setup effort.

\subsection{Future Research Directions}

This work suggests several research extensions:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}

\item
  \textbf{Denormalized capacity analysis:} Re-aggregating raw HERE speed and free-flow speed data to compute absolute delay (\(\Delta t = L \times (1/v - 1/v_f)\)), enabling direct testing of whether high-capacity roads carry more total delay despite identical jam factors
\item
  \textbf{Temporal capacity interactions:} Examining whether capacity constraints become relevant only during specific peak periods, using time-stratified bottleneck analysis
\item
  Integration with public transport data to assess multimodal impacts on congestion distribution
\item
  Machine learning approaches for congestion prediction combining temporal features with network topology
\item
  Extension to additional Indonesian cities for broader comparative analysis
\item
  Intervention evaluation using before-after analysis of infrastructure changes
\item
  \textbf{Microscopic bottleneck analysis:} Using intersection-level data to test whether signal timing and turning movements create localized capacity effects invisible in segment-level analysis
\end{enumerate}



\section{Conclusions}

This study presents an open-source pipeline for spatiotemporal traffic congestion analysis that integrates HERE Traffic API data collection, OSMnx network analysis, and PySAL geostatistics into a reproducible, city-agnostic framework. We demonstrate the pipeline across three Indonesian metropolitan areas using high-resolution traffic flow data spanning 11 months and 264 million observations.

\subsection{Pipeline Contribution}

The primary contribution is the \textbf{open-source analysis pipeline} itself (\url{https://github.com/firmanhadi21/traffic-analyses}), which:

\begin{itemize}
\item
  Automates the full workflow from data collection through publication-ready outputs
\item
  Requires only a bounding box and API key to replicate for any city with HERE API coverage
\item
  Combines commercial traffic data with open geospatial tools (OSMnx, PySAL, GeoPandas) in a modular, extensible architecture
\item
  Introduces a novel graph-based capacity drop detection module for systematic bottleneck analysis
\item
  Is validated across three cities spanning a 20x population range and distinct urban morphologies
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Primary Empirical Finding: Temporal Dominance}

\textbf{Relative congestion (jam factor) is fundamentally a temporal phenomenon, not a spatial one.} Time-of-day explains 15-24\% of jam factor variance ($\eta^2$ from ANOVA), while spatial predictors---network centrality, POI density, and road capacity---explain less than 0.02\% combined. This 1,000-4,000x difference in explanatory power demonstrates that:

\begin{itemize}

\item
  \textbf{When} people travel matters far more than \textbf{where} relative congestion occurs
\item
  Relative congestion results from \textbf{synchronized travel demand}, not problematic locations or capacity constraints
\item
  Static infrastructure characteristics---including road hierarchy and capacity transitions---cannot predict relative congestion patterns
\end{itemize}

An important caveat is that the jam factor normalizes speed to free-flow conditions, which by design removes absolute capacity effects. Whether temporal dominance also holds for absolute delay metrics (total vehicle-hours lost) remains an open question requiring raw speed and volume data. Nevertheless, the finding has clear implications for demand management policy, since the jam factor captures the user-experienced congestion that travelers respond to.

\subsection{Secondary Findings}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Evening peak dominance:} Congestion during 16:00-19:00 exceeds daily averages by approximately 40\% across all three cities, with Jakarta reaching a mean jam factor of 2.01
\item
  \textbf{Local clustering despite global randomness:} While global Moran's I shows no significant spatial autocorrelation, LISA identifies meaningful local hotspots (10\% of segments) representing peak-hour capacity bottlenecks
\item
  \textbf{Scale effects:} Larger cities exhibit both higher absolute congestion and greater peak/off-peak differentials (Jakarta 30.8\%, Semarang 25.6\%), suggesting urban scale amplifies temporal variability
\item
  \textbf{Network topology irrelevance:} Betweenness centrality shows near-zero correlation with congestion (r < 0.02), contradicting assumptions that topologically important roads experience more congestion
\item
  \textbf{Capacity constraint irrelevance:} Neither aggregate road capacity (Cohen's d = -0.03 to -0.07, all p > 0.05), spatial capacity drops (78-1,443 detected; proximity p = 0.10-0.83), nor local bottleneck gradients (| d| < 0.05) predict congestion levels. The bottleneck hypothesis---that congestion concentrates where road capacity decreases---is not supported by the data
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Policy Implications}

These findings suggest important considerations for traffic management, with the caveat that they are based on relative congestion metrics and should be complemented by absolute delay analysis before informing major infrastructure investment decisions:

\begin{itemize}

\item
  \textbf{Demand management} (staggered hours, flexible work, congestion pricing) should be considered alongside infrastructure expansion, given the strong temporal dominance in relative congestion
\item
  \textbf{Temporal targeting} of resources to the 16:00-19:00 peak period is likely to maximize impact regardless of metric choice
\item
  \textbf{Infrastructure decisions} should not rely solely on normalized congestion metrics; absolute delay and volume data are needed to evaluate whether road expansion addresses total delay even if relative congestion remains temporally driven
\item
  \textbf{Capacity transitions} (e.g., motorway-to-arterial merges) do not create measurable relative congestion signatures in jam factor data, though this does not preclude absolute throughput effects
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Contribution}

This research contributes both a reusable open-source pipeline and novel empirical evidence demonstrating that urban congestion is primarily a temporal coordination failure rather than a spatial infrastructure or capacity constraint problem. The pipeline's city-agnostic design---requiring only a bounding box and API key---enables researchers and practitioners in any city with HERE API coverage to replicate and extend this analysis. The novel graph-based capacity drop detection framework provides a replicable method for testing the bottleneck hypothesis in other urban contexts. The finding that time explains 1,000x more variance than space, corroborated by the failure of three independent capacity tests, has significant implications for transportation policy worldwide. All source code, analysis scripts, and aggregated datasets are available at \url{https://github.com/firmanhadi21/traffic-analyses}.
