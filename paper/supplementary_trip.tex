\section{Spatial Weight Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:s1}

To confirm that the null global spatial autocorrelation finding reported in the main text is robust, we computed Moran's I under five spatial weight specifications for each city: K-nearest neighbours with $k \in \{4, 8, 12\}$ and distance-band weights at 500\,m and 1000\,m thresholds.

\begin{table}[!htbp]
\centering
\caption{Moran's I under alternative spatial weight specifications (overall mean jam factor). All 15 city--weight combinations yield non-significant results ($p > 0.05$), confirming the absence of global spatial autocorrelation.}
\label{tab:s1}
\begin{tabular}{lccccc}
\toprule
City & KNN $k{=}4$ & KNN $k{=}8$ & KNN $k{=}12$ & Dist 500\,m & Dist 1000\,m \\
\midrule
Jakarta  & 0.001 ($p{=}0.86$) & 0.003 ($p{=}0.46$) & 0.005 ($p{=}0.12$) & $-0.009$ ($p{=}0.14$) & $-0.005$ ($p{=}0.33$) \\
Bandung  & 0.004 ($p{=}0.74$) & 0.007 ($p{=}0.35$) & 0.009 ($p{=}0.20$) & 0.007 ($p{=}0.63$)    & $-0.000$ ($p{=}0.99$) \\
Semarang & 0.008 ($p{=}0.67$) & $-0.004$ ($p{=}0.84$) & 0.003 ($p{=}0.77$) & 0.003 ($p{=}0.89$) & $-0.004$ ($p{=}0.88$) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The distance-band weights at 500\,m and 1000\,m provide implicit correlogram information. Both distance thresholds yield non-significant Moran's I across all cities, indicating that spatial autocorrelation is absent at both local (neighbouring segments) and broader spatial scales.

\section{H3 Hexagonal Aggregation: Extended Results}
\label{sec:s4}

This section provides extended results from the H3 hexagonal scale-sensitivity analysis described in Section 4.4 and reported in Section 5.6 of the main text. The Uber H3 hierarchical geospatial indexing system (Brodsky, 2018) was used to aggregate road-segment-level variables into regular hexagonal bins at two resolutions, testing whether the null correlations observed at the segment level reflect genuine absence of relationships or a modifiable areal unit problem (MAUP) artefact.

\end{table}

The higher segment density per hexagon in Jakarta reflects its denser road network. At resolution 8, each hexagon contains sufficient observations (31,000--112,000) for stable mean estimates.

Jakarta's emergence of significant spatial autocorrelation at the hexagonal scale suggests that its congestion patterns have coherent neighbourhood-level structure---hexagons with elevated congestion tend to be surrounded by similarly elevated neighbours---that is masked by within-segment noise at finer resolutions. Importantly, this clustering is \emph{not} explained by POI density or betweenness centrality, both of which remain uncorrelated with congestion even at the hex scale where autocorrelation is detected. The drivers of Jakarta's weak but consistent neighbourhood clustering warrant further investigation, with candidate explanatory variables including land-use mix, population density, and access to public transit.

\section{Spatial Regression Results}
\label{sec:s6}

To properly account for spatial dependence, Spatial Lag and Spatial Error models (via PySAL's \texttt{spreg}) were estimated alongside OLS multiple regression. The dependent variable is mean evening-peak jam factor; independent variables are edge betweenness centrality and POI density within a 500\,m buffer.

\begin{table}[!htbp]
\centering
\caption{Spatial regression results: jam factor $\sim$ betweenness centrality $+$ POI density. Across all model specifications, $R^2$ values are negligible ($<$0.3\%), confirming that spatial predictors explain virtually none of the congestion variance.}
\label{tab:s8}
\small
\begin{tabular}{llcccccl}
\toprule
City & Model & $R^2$ & $\beta$ (betw.) & $p$ & $\beta$ (POI) & $p$ & Spatial param. \\
\midrule
\multirow{3}{*}{Jakarta}
 & OLS           & 0.003\% & $-0.048$ & 0.635 & 0.000010  & 0.704 & ---              \\
 & Spatial Lag   & 0.006\% & $-0.337$ & 0.168 & $-0.000005$ & 0.899 & $\rho = 2.731$   \\
 & Spatial Error & 0.003\% & $-0.051$ & 0.615 & 0.000009  & 0.708 & $\lambda = 0.012$ \\
\midrule
\multirow{3}{*}{Bandung}
 & OLS           & 0.260\% & 0.270    & 0.005 & $-0.000010$ & 0.803 & ---              \\
 & Spatial Lag   & 0.128\% & 0.231    & 0.064 & $-0.000000$ & 0.996 & $\rho = 0.350$   \\
 & Spatial Error & 0.260\% & 0.269    & 0.005 & $-0.000010$ & 0.817 & $\lambda = 0.023$ \\
\midrule
\multirow{3}{*}{Semarang}
 & OLS           & 0.004\% & $-0.017$ & 0.894 & $-0.000007$ & 0.887 & ---              \\
 & Spatial Lag   & 0.013\% & 0.033    & 0.866 & 0.000055  & 0.706 & $\rho = 2.568$   \\
 & Spatial Error & 0.004\% & $-0.018$ & 0.886 & $-0.000008$ & 0.867 & $\lambda = -0.034$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Bandung's OLS betweenness coefficient is statistically significant ($p{=}0.005$) but trivially small in magnitude ($R^2 = 0.26\%$). This significance vanishes in the Spatial Lag model ($p{=}0.064$), suggesting it is partially attributable to spatial dependence rather than a genuine centrality effect. The spatial autoregressive parameters ($\rho$, $\lambda$) are small, consistent with the weak global spatial autocorrelation reported in the main text and Table~\ref{tab:s1}.
